# Kernel 02 — Orientation

> *"We are made for cooperation, like feet, like hands, like eyelids, like the rows of upper and lower teeth."*
> — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, Book 8

---

## The claim

You have no business engaging with others until you have asked what you are bringing.

Current platforms open immediately to the feed. No pause, no reckoning, no question about your intention or your role. The architecture assumes you are ready. You may not be.

---

## The thinker

Marcus Aurelius began every day with private examination - not inspiration, discipline. The Meditations were never intended to be read. They were a daily practice of reorientation: what am I here for, what do I owe the people I will encounter, what am I carrying that is not mine to carry into this space.

His argument was not philosophical preference. He believed cooperation was the natural condition of human beings - to act against it, to enter interaction without examining your intention, was to act against your own nature.

He also wrote for no audience. That is worth noting. The practice of self-examination was separable from performance. The question was asked privately before anything was offered publicly.

---

## The friction

Before you can interact, you are presented with one question - rotating through seven Aurelius-rooted prompts. You answer it. Briefly, honestly. The answer stays visible as an anchor while you say what you came to say.

The questions include:

- *What do you owe the people you are about to encounter?*
- *What are you bringing into this space - and is it yours to bring?*
- *Are you here to cooperate or to perform?*
- *What would you want from others today - and are you prepared to offer it first?*
- *What assumption are you carrying in that you have not examined?*
- *Is what you are about to say in service of connection or in service of being seen?*
- *What would it mean to leave this space better than you found it?*

You cannot proceed without answering. The answer is not validated for content - only for genuine engagement. You cannot type two words and move on.

---

## What the experiment measures

Whether naming your orientation before you enter a social space changes how you behave in it.

Does the quality of what you say shift when you have first answered what you owe? Does holding your own answer visible while you interact slow the reactive impulse? Does it make you more cooperative and less performative?

---

## What it cannot prove

The questions carry the Aurelius philosophy embedded in their framing. A user who has not read the Meditations will still feel the reorientation - but they may also resist it as friction without understanding why it is there.

This kernel also cannot measure what happens after. Whether the orientation holds once someone leaves the prototype and enters a real platform is unknown. The experiment ends at the boundary of the tool.

---

## How to extend this

- Let users write their own orientation question - does authoring the question change the quality of the answer?
- Test whether the anchor staying visible during interaction has measurable effect versus answering and proceeding without it
- Build a version where the question is sent to you privately at a random point during your interaction - mid-scroll, mid-thread
- Pair with the witness kernel: orientation before entering, witness before responding
